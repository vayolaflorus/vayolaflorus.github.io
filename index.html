Supporting nonprofit and public-sector teams to use technology responsibly.

If you’re responsible for programs and people, you’re likely under pressure to use more technology and AI — even when time, staff capacity, and support are limited.

I support nonprofit and public-sector organizations by building staff capacity and supporting public-facing programs that help community members build practical digital skills.

What I do

I support organizations in two connected ways. Some teams need one. Many need both.

1. Building staff capacity

Who this is for

Program managers, frontline staff, and leadership teams who are expected to use new digital tools or AI as part of their work.

What I support

  • Practical training for staff
  • Workshops adapted to real roles and workflows
  • Support as teams adjust to new tools
  • Space to slow down and ask questions

Why it matters

When staff feel prepared, decisions feel safer and programs run more smoothly.

2. Supporting public-facing programs

Who this is for

Organizations running programs that help community members build digital skills or access technology.

What I support

  • Program design and planning
  • Facilitating or supporting participant training
  • Adapting content for different language levels
  • Making sure programs work in real life

Why it matters

Programs work better when they reflect real needs, real constraints, and real people.

Because I work across staff capacity and program delivery, I help organizations move from good intentions to real impact.

Who I work with

I work primarily with people who have responsibility for staff, programs, and outcomes, including:

  • Program managers
  • Executive directors
  • Nonprofit leaders
  • Development directors
  • Public-sector teams

About

I work with nonprofit and public-sector organizations that are navigating increased pressure to use technology and AI responsibly.

I’ve seen how often teams are asked to adopt new tools without enough time, guidance, or support — and how that pressure affects both staff and the people programs are meant to serve.

My approach is practical and people-centered. I focus on how technology shows up in everyday work — in meetings, programs, classrooms, and community spaces.

I believe technology should support staff, increase access, and strengthen programs — not overwhelm people or create new barriers.
